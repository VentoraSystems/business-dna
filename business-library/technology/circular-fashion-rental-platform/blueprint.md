# Business Blueprint — Loopwear Rental Co.

_Educational business-building guidance only. This document describes
how to build and run a clothing rental subscription platform as a
business. Each `##` heading corresponds to one of the 25 sections in
`features/blueprint`'s v2 canonical structure._

## Executive Summary

Loopwear Rental Co. is a clothing rental subscription platform. Members
pay a tiered monthly fee for rotating access to a shared wardrobe of
curated garments — special-occasion wear and versatile work-capsule
pieces — wearing an item, then returning or swapping it rather than
owning it. Ownership never transfers: the same physical garments are
rented out repeatedly to many different members, cycled through a
professional reverse-logistics facility (inspection, industrial
cleaning, minor repair, and re-listing) after every single return, with
an optional buy-out price for members who want to keep a specific piece
permanently.

## Entrepreneur Fit

Best suited to `theOperator` or `theVisionary` archetype, with at least
three years of relevant experience — a founder who can run a tight
reverse-logistics cycle at consistent quality on every single return,
paired with the circular-economy vision to build a rental-fleet
business that never transfers ownership.

## Business Overview

A rental subscription business built on a capitalized, depreciating
garment-fleet asset rather than inventory sold once and done. Members
select a subscription tier (by items held at once / swaps per month),
receive curated garments, and return them for the reverse-logistics
cycle before the piece is re-listed to the next member — access, not
ownership, is the product.

## Market Intelligence

Demand comes from consumers who want variety and occasion-specific
garments (weddings, galas, work-capsule pieces) without the cost or
closet burden of owning them outright. The competitive set includes
one-time-sale sustainable-fashion brands elsewhere in this library
(which sell a garment once and are done with it) and conventional
retail/fast fashion (cheaper per-purchase but no access model or
reverse-logistics quality guarantee).

## Customer Intelligence

Primary customer type is B2C: consumers renting special-occasion pieces
for one-off events, and professionals building a rotating work-capsule
wardrobe without repeated purchases, both drawn to the cost-per-wear
economics and willing to trust a rental garment's cleanliness because
of a verifiable reverse-logistics process.

## Offer Architecture

The core offer is tiered subscription access to a curated shared
wardrobe (items held at once / swaps per month), supported by a
professional reverse-logistics cycle after every return and an optional
buy-out program for members who bond with a specific piece.

## Revenue Architecture

Recurring subscription MRR builds gradually as members must trust
rental garment quality before committing, and the fleet itself must be
capitalized before it can generate revenue — slow revenue speed, offset
over time by compounding subscription revenue plus incremental buy-out
sales and corporate partnership fees.

## Pricing Strategy

Starter tier ~€39/month (2 items held at once, 1 swap/month); Curator
tier ~€69/month (4 items, 2 swaps/month); Unlimited tier ~€99/month (6
items, unlimited swaps); buy-out pricing at roughly 40-60% of original
retail depending on garment condition and rental history.

## Marketing System

Positioning: "wear more, own less." Primary channel is circular-economy
content and sustainability-community marketing (cost-per-wear,
landfill-diversion messaging); performance social and member-referral
marketing is secondary; corporate work-capsule partnership outreach is
experimental.

## Sales System

An online storefront presents tiered subscription plans after a short
style quiz, with a buy-out offer surfaced post-rental — a roughly
5-day consideration cycle given the recurring-commitment decision, plus
direct outreach to companies for corporate work-capsule accounts.

## Operations System

Core recurring processes: garment inspection, industrial cleaning, and
minor repair after every single return; rental-fleet inventory
management (rental count, condition, wear-life, retirement
scheduling); subscription-tier and swap-scheduling management; and
curated garment sourcing and cataloging.

## Technology Stack

Built on Booqable for rental-fleet inventory management, Recharge for
subscription billing, Klaviyo for email/SMS marketing, and Loop Returns
as an optional reverse-logistics platform.

## Automation Opportunities

Subscription billing, swap scheduling, and the member portal are
automated; the physical inspection, cleaning, and repair cycle remains
deliberately hands-on and human-verified before any garment is
re-listed.

## Team Structure

Not solo-founder-friendly — running a physical garment-processing and
cleaning facility alongside fleet management requires a team from
launch, with garment-processing and inspection staff as the first
hires.

## Financial Overview

Startup budget of roughly €35,000-€65,000 covers the initial rental-
fleet garment acquisition, the garment-processing and cleaning facility
build-out, and subscription platform setup — a Medium-High budget tier;
target monthly income ranges €12,000-€30,000 with an estimated 14-month
path to break-even.

## KPIs

Tracked against subscription-driven MRR growth, subscriber retention
and churn, gross margin against reverse-logistics and fleet-
depreciation costs, and corporate-partnership close rate.

## Risk Analysis

High difficulty with moderate AI-resistance — the three biggest risks
are garment damage/loss inflating reverse-logistics costs, fleet-
utilization rate falling below the break-even threshold, and any lapse
in cleaning/inspection quality breaking member trust.

## Competitive Advantages

A disciplined reverse-logistics cycle builds member trust in
cleanliness that looser rental competitors can't match, recurring
subscription MRR compounds predictably unlike one-off purchase bursts,
and the optional buy-out program captures incremental revenue without
requiring the core model to become ownership-based.

## Launch Strategy

Acquire the initial curated rental fleet and build out the garment-
processing and cleaning facility, then validate tier pricing and swap
cadence with a small early member base at standard pricing before
scaling the fleet.

## 90-Day Action Plan

Weeks 1-5: acquire the pilot fleet, build the reverse-logistics
facility, and launch the subscription platform. Weeks 6-9: validate
with an early member base and stress-test the reverse-logistics cycle.
Weeks 10-13: launch the full catalog publicly and begin cost-per-wear
content marketing.

## Scaling Strategy

Expand the rental fleet and reverse-logistics processing capacity
together in step with confirmed demand, then optimize fleet-utilization
rate as the primary lever on unit economics, then expand into corporate
work-capsule partnerships as a stabilizing secondary channel.

## Exit Opportunities

A documented rental-fleet asset register, working reverse-logistics
processes, a subscriber base, and corporate partnerships make this
sellable to a larger circular-fashion or lifestyle-rental platform.

## AI Recommendations

Prioritize AI tools for fleet-utilization/replenishment forecasting and
personalized style/swap recommendations — implementation accelerators —
while keeping the physical inspection, cleaning, and repair cycle fully
human-verified.
