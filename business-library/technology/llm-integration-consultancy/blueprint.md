# Business Blueprint — Substrate AI

_Educational business-building guidance only. This document describes how
to build and grow a bespoke LLM-integration engineering consultancy as a
business. Each `##` heading corresponds to one of the 25 sections in
`features/blueprint`'s v2 canonical structure._

## Executive Summary

Substrate AI is a boutique software-engineering consultancy that designs
and ships bespoke LLM integrations directly into a client's own
proprietary systems and data: custom RAG pipelines over an internal
knowledge base, fine-tuning or deep prompt engineering against a
proprietary dataset, and LLM capability embedded straight into a client's
existing ERP, case-management system, or internal tool. It is sold as
fixed-scope, project-based engineering work — a paid discovery sprint,
then a milestone-billed build, then an optional maintenance retainer —
not a strategy diagnostic and not a shared, self-serve product. It's for
a hands-on senior engineer with real delivery track record, not a
strategist or a platform operator.

## Entrepreneur Fit

Best suited to `theBuilder` or `theSpecialist` archetype, with at least
six years of hands-on engineering experience, comfortable scoping a
fixed-price build against a client's messy proprietary data and earning
the trust needed to work inside a client's own security perimeter.

## Business Overview

A boutique, engineering-led consultancy serving mid-market and enterprise
companies with a specific, proprietary use case that doesn't fit an
off-the-shelf AI product — a law firm wanting an internal RAG system over
its own case files, a manufacturer wanting an LLM copilot over its
internal parts catalog, an insurer wanting document intelligence over its
own claims history.

## Market Intelligence

Demand is driven by companies that have already concluded generic AI
chat tools and off-the-shelf SaaS products can't reach their real,
proprietary data and internal systems, and need custom engineering
instead. The competitive set ranges from strategy-only AI advisories
(who diagnose but don't build), to narrow productized AI tools (who solve
one shape of problem for everyone), to large systems integrators (slow,
expensive, generalist). A boutique that does nothing but bespoke LLM
integration engineering sits deliberately between those, closer to the
client's own engineering team than to a consulting firm.

## Customer Intelligence

Primary customer type is B2B: CTOs, Heads of Engineering, and functional
leaders (General Counsel, VP Operations, Head of Claims) at mid-market
and enterprise companies with a large, valuable, and genuinely
proprietary body of internal data or software that a generic AI product
can't touch. The common pain point is sitting on a knowledge base, case
history, or internal system that clearly has AI-shaped value locked
inside it, with no internal team able to build the integration safely and
well.

## Offer Architecture

The core offer is a fixed-scope integration build — a paid discovery and
data-audit sprint, followed by a milestone-billed RAG pipeline, fine-tuning,
or in-system LLM embedding project — with an optional monthly maintenance
and model-upkeep retainer once the system ships.

## Revenue Architecture

Revenue arrives in lumpy, milestone-based payments tied to each
fixed-scope integration build rather than a smooth ramp, gradually
smoothed by a growing maintenance-retainer base as delivered integrations
accumulate.

## Pricing Strategy

A paid discovery and data-audit sprint precedes every fixed-price quote,
protecting margin against the near-universal reality that a client's
proprietary data is messier than it looked in the sales conversation —
and giving the client a real technical spec, not an estimate, before they
commit to the build.

## Marketing System

Positioning: "LLM systems built directly on your own data and inside your
own software — not a chatbot bolted on top." Primary channel is named
technical case studies and referrals from past clients' technical and
functional leaders; targeted outbound to CTOs with a specific proprietary
use case and technical-conference presence build secondary and
experimental awareness.

## Sales System

Consultative technical scoping — a paid discovery and data-audit sprint
precedes every fixed-price integration-build proposal, with a roughly
50-day cycle from first conversation to signed build.

## Operations System

Core recurring processes: a paid discovery and data-audit sprint, a
two-week-sprint RAG/fine-tuning build cycle with live demos against real
or sandboxed client data, a security review before any production
integration into the client's live systems, and an optional post-launch
maintenance retainer covering monitoring and model upkeep.

## Technology Stack

Built on a RAG orchestration framework (LangChain/LlamaIndex), a managed
vector database, and standard engineering tooling (GitHub, Linear) —
deliberately model-agnostic so no single foundation-model vendor's
pricing or deprecation timeline can strand a client's integration.

## Automation Opportunities

AI coding assistants accelerate pipeline boilerplate and evaluation-
harness scaffolding, and an automated evaluation harness continuously
scores retrieval accuracy and hallucination rate against a held-out
question set from the client's own domain — catching regressions before
the client does, without automating away the bespoke engineering judgment
itself.

## Team Structure

Requires a small senior engineering team rather than being purely
solo-founder-friendly — the founder can personally deliver the first one
or two builds, but sustained delivery against enterprise proprietary data
and security requirements needs a second senior hire early.

## Financial Overview

Startup budget of roughly €22,000-€40,000 covers company formation and
data-processing agreement templates, RAG/vector-database tooling, an
initial contract-engineer retainer, and technical sales collateral;
target monthly income ranges €20,000-€55,000 with an estimated 9-month
path to break-even.

## KPIs

Tracked against discovery-sprint close rate, gross margin per
fixed-scope build, maintenance-retainer conversion (customer retention),
and retainer MRR growth.

## Risk Analysis

High difficulty and moderate AI-resistance: AI coding assistants
gradually commoditize simpler integration work, but deep, bespoke
engineering against a client's messy, sensitive proprietary data and
existing systems resists full automation. The three biggest risks are
mishandling a client's proprietary/sensitive data, proprietary data
turning out messier than the sales conversation suggested (blowing up a
fixed-price quote), and tight coupling to a foundation-model API that
gets deprecated.

## Competitive Advantages

A paid discovery-and-data-audit sprint that de-risks fixed-price scoping,
deep hands-on engineering credibility that a strategy-only advisory or
generalist agency can't match, and a model-agnostic architecture that
protects both client and firm from foundation-model vendor churn.

## Launch Strategy

Form the company and draft data-processing agreement templates first,
then sell the first discovery-and-data-audit sprint through a direct,
technically credible outbound approach or a personal engineering network
referral — technical proof, not volume, opens the first door.

## 90-Day Action Plan

Weeks 1-4: form the company, finalize MSA/SOW and data-processing
agreement templates, and select the core RAG/vector-database delivery
stack. Weeks 5-9: sell and deliver the first paid discovery and
data-audit sprint, then begin the first fixed-scope build in two-week
sprints. Weeks 10-13: ship the first integration's first production
milestone, publish a case study once the client signs off, and offer the
maintenance retainer proactively at handoff.

## Scaling Strategy

Scale by hiring a second senior ML/backend engineer to remove the
key-person delivery bottleneck, then specialize into one or two vertical
niches — legal case-file RAG, insurer claims-history document
intelligence — to command premium fixed-scope pricing and build reusable
(but still bespoke) internal accelerators per vertical.

## Exit Opportunities

A documented client book, active maintenance retainers, and a portfolio
of named technical case studies and integration playbooks are attractive
to a larger engineering studio or systems integrator looking to acquire a
proven applied-AI delivery capability.

## AI Recommendations

Use AI coding assistants and automated evaluation harnesses to accelerate
delivery and continuously monitor retrieval quality, never to substitute
for the engineering judgment of scoping a client's proprietary,
security-sensitive integration — the entire value proposition rests on
hands-on, accountable human engineering that a client trusts precisely
because a real person owns the build.
