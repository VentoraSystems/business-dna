# Business Blueprint — Solstice Jewelry Co.

_Educational business-building guidance only. This document describes how
to build and grow a direct-to-consumer fine jewelry brand as a business.
Each `##` heading corresponds to one of the 25 sections in
`features/blueprint`'s v2 canonical structure._

## Executive Summary

Solstice Jewelry Co. is a direct-to-consumer fine jewelry brand built
around ethically-sourced, lab-grown pieces designed for daily wear, sold
through owned ecommerce channels rather than traditional retail. Unlike a
service business, this is a physical-goods business where capital sits in
inventory and cash flow is gated by production cycles, not by client
availability. It's for someone with a genuine design point of view who
also has the operational discipline to manage sourcing, inventory, and
fulfillment.

## Entrepreneur Fit

Best suited to `theVisionary` or `theOperator` archetype, with at least
two years of experience in product design, retail, or ecommerce
operations. The strongest `creator` archetype match of any business in
this category — this work rewards genuine design taste as much as
operational execution.

## Business Overview

A direct-to-consumer fine jewelry brand serving design-conscious
professional women who buy fine jewelry for themselves or as a considered
gift, with a core differentiator of ethically-sourced, lab-grown materials
and a risk-free try-at-home purchase experience.

## Market Intelligence

Demand comes from customers who want fine jewelry with genuine daily-wear
design and a verifiable ethical materials story, not mass-market costume
jewelry or the markup and formality of traditional mined-diamond luxury
houses. The competitive set includes both ends of that spectrum — neither
offers this brand's specific combination of accessible pricing and
verifiable sourcing.

## Customer Intelligence

Primary customer type is B2C: design-conscious professional women aged
28-45. Common pain points are uncertainty about sizing when buying jewelry
online without trying it on, and distrust of vague "ethically sourced"
claims from brands with no verifiable sourcing story.

## Offer Architecture

The core offer is direct-to-consumer product sales across tiered price
points, always backed by a free 30-day try-at-home return window with
complimentary resizing, with limited-edition collection drops and
corporate gifting bulk orders as secondary revenue streams.

## Revenue Architecture

Revenue builds slowly at first, as the first inventory run sells through
and initial audience trust is established, then accelerates as
repeat-purchase and gifting-season cohorts compound on top of new-customer
acquisition — a materially different growth curve from a service business
that can sign a client in week one.

## Pricing Strategy

Tiered price points from $150 to $800 across everyday and statement
pieces, with limited-edition collection drops creating purchase urgency —
direct-to-consumer pricing bypasses traditional jewelry-retail markup
layers while still protecting margin against paid-acquisition costs.

## Marketing System

Positioning: "fine jewelry, made to keep — not to trend." Primary channel
is micro-influencer and UGC seeding showing pieces worn in daily life;
Meta and Google Shopping ads convert warmed audiences, and email/SMS
lifecycle flows drive repeat purchases.

## Sales System

A single-step online checkout, backed by a free 30-day try-at-home return
window and complimentary resizing, removes the biggest hesitation in
buying fine jewelry sight unseen — a roughly 3-day cycle from first
visit to purchase.

## Operations System

Core recurring processes: supplier sourcing and quality control for
lab-grown materials, product photography and catalog updates for each new
collection, order fulfillment through a third-party logistics partner, and
customer service for sizing and returns.

## Technology Stack

Built on Shopify for the storefront and order management, Klaviyo for
email/SMS lifecycle marketing, and Loox for AI-assisted review and
UGC collection — a leaner, more consumer-facing stack than the
B2B-service businesses elsewhere in this library.

## Automation Opportunities

AI-assisted product photography editing accelerates catalog production
for each new collection; an AI-assisted customer service chatbot handles
common sizing and shipping questions before escalating anything complex to
a human.

## Team Structure

Highly solo-founder-friendly at launch, relying on a third-party logistics
partner for warehousing and shipping; a customer-service contractor is
typically the first hire once order volume grows past what the founder can
personally manage.

## Financial Overview

Startup budget of roughly €15,000-€40,000 — the largest single line item
is the first inventory production run, unlike a service business; target
monthly income ranges €8,000-€30,000 with an estimated 9-month path to
break-even.

## KPIs

Tracked against blended customer acquisition cost, customer lifetime
value through repeat purchases, gross margin protection against rising ad
costs, and customer retention via repeat-purchase rate.

## Risk Analysis

Moderate difficulty and moderate AI-resistance — physical product design
and sourcing relationships are harder for AI to replicate than pure
content work, but AI-generated product photography is lowering the
barrier for lookalike competitors. The three biggest risks are capital
tied up in unsold inventory, rising paid-ad costs eroding margins, and
lower-cost knockoff pieces undercutting on price once a design proves
popular.

## Competitive Advantages

A free 30-day try-at-home window with complimentary resizing that removes
the biggest online-purchase hesitation, a verifiable ethically-sourced,
lab-grown materials story that differentiates from both mass-market and
traditional luxury competitors, and a direct customer relationship that
enables fast iteration on which styles to restock versus retire.

## Launch Strategy

Finalize the first collection design and materials sourcing, produce a
small pilot batch to validate demand with an early-access list, then
launch the full collection on the live storefront once pricing and design
appeal are confirmed.

## 90-Day Action Plan

Weeks 1-4: finalize sourcing and produce the pilot batch. Weeks 5-9: sell
the pilot batch to an early-access list and launch the full first
collection on the storefront. Weeks 10-13: drive initial traffic through
influencer and UGC seeding and convert the first customer wave into
repeat buyers through post-purchase email flows.

## Scaling Strategy

Scale by identifying and doubling down on the best-selling, highest
repeat-purchase-rate SKUs, then expand into an adjacent product line (such
as a bridal collection) and formalize wholesale partnerships with
independent boutiques once the direct-to-consumer channel is proven.

## Exit Opportunities

A documented customer list, repeat-purchase data, and wholesale
relationships are attractive to a larger consumer-goods company looking to
acquire a proven direct-to-consumer brand and customer base rather than
build one from scratch.

## AI Recommendations

Prioritize AI tools for product photography editing and first-line
customer service triage — implementation accelerators — while keeping
product design, sourcing decisions, and brand voice fully human-led.
