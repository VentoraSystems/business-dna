# Business Blueprint — Basalt Coffee Roasters

_Educational business-building guidance only. This document describes
how to build and run a neighborhood specialty coffee shop as a business.
Each `##` heading corresponds to one of the 25 sections in
`features/blueprint`'s v2 canonical structure._

## Executive Summary

Basalt Coffee Roasters is a neighborhood specialty coffee shop built
around counter-service espresso, brewed coffee, a curated single-origin
bean program, and a small grab-and-go pastry case, serving a quick
daily-habit occasion rather than a dining occasion. It's for a hands-on
operator ready to work the counter and build a local following — the
lowest capital and fastest cash-collection cycle of the two physical
food-service businesses in this library.

## Entrepreneur Fit

Best suited to `theOperator` or `theHustler` archetype, with real
barista or food-service experience shortening the ramp considerably.
No formal license is required, but early-morning hours and hands-on
counter work demand real physical stamina and consistency.

## Business Overview

A counter-service coffee shop delivering espresso and brewed drinks,
packaged whole-bean retail, and a small wholesale pastry case, staffed
by the founding operator alongside at least one barista, growing through
foot traffic, local social content, and word of mouth.

## Market Intelligence

Demand comes from commuters and neighborhood residents seeking a daily
coffee stop. The competitive set includes large chain coffee stores
(consistent but impersonal, less specialty-quality) and convenience-store
coffee (cheaper, lower quality, no craft or counter relationship).

## Customer Intelligence

Primary customer type is B2C: local commuters and remote workers aged
22-45 who want specialty-quality coffee as part of a daily ritual. The
core pain point is choosing between an impersonal chain and a
lower-quality convenience option.

## Offer Architecture

The core offer is espresso and brewed-coffee drinks served at the
counter, supported by packaged whole-bean retail sales and a small
wholesale pastry case as add-on revenue.

## Revenue Architecture

Every transaction is a small, immediate cash or card sale at the
counter — the fastest cash-collection cycle of any physical-location
business in this library, with no invoicing or payment delay.

## Pricing Strategy

Per-item counter pricing (e.g. €3.50 latte, €2.50 drip coffee, €4
pastry), plus €14 retail bags of whole-bean coffee.

## Marketing System

Positioning: "your daily ritual, done right." Primary channel is
street-level foot traffic and signage; local social media content builds
secondary awareness, with a loyalty punch-card or app as an experimental
retention channel.

## Sales System

A walk-in customer orders at the counter and receives their drink within
minutes — an impulse, habit-driven purchase with no consideration cycle,
the most immediate sales dynamic in this library.

## Operations System

Core recurring processes: early-morning opening prep, counter service
through the morning rush, daily perishable-inventory ordering, and
end-of-day point-of-sale reconciliation and cleaning.

## Technology Stack

Built on Square for point-of-sale and payments, with 7shifts for staff
scheduling and Fivestars as an optional loyalty and retention tool.

## Automation Opportunities

Point-of-sale reporting and loyalty-app tracking are automated;
AI-assisted demand forecasting suggests daily bean, milk, and pastry
order quantities to reduce waste.

## Team Structure

Not solo-friendly — requires at least one barista from day one to cover
opening hours; the first growth hire is typically a shift lead to extend
opening hours or cover peak-hour capacity.

## Financial Overview

Startup budget of roughly €10,000-€30,000 covers the espresso machine,
grinders, counter buildout, and initial inventory — a Medium budget
tier, well below the full-kitchen capital required for a restaurant;
target monthly income ranges €10,000-€26,000 with an estimated 12-month
path to break-even.

## KPIs

Tracked against daily/weekly regular-customer retention, gross margin
against bean/milk/pastry cost of goods, foot-traffic-to-regular
conversion, and regular-customer attrition.

## Risk Analysis

Moderate difficulty with high AI-resistance — the in-person counter
experience requires a human barista. The three biggest risks are thin
per-transaction margins against fixed lease and labor costs, heavy
dependence on the specific location's foot traffic, and perishable
inventory waste risk.

## Competitive Advantages

High repeat-visit frequency builds meaningful customer lifetime value
despite small individual tickets, recession-resistant demand for an
affordable daily indulgence, and a genuine specialty-coffee identity
that differentiates against both chain and convenience-store coffee.

## Launch Strategy

Confirm real foot traffic at the target location, secure financing and
complete the counter buildout, then validate the concept through a
pop-up or farmers-market stand before committing to the full lease.

## 90-Day Action Plan

Weeks 1-6: finalize buildout, permits, and equipment installation.
Weeks 7-8: run a soft opening for friends, family, and neighbors. Weeks
9-13: launch the full public opening with local social content and
signage, and begin building the daily-regular base.

## Scaling Strategy

Build a stable base of daily regulars at the first location first, grow
packaged bean retail and pastry add-on revenue as a share of total
sales, then evaluate a second location once the first is consistently
profitable.

## Exit Opportunities

Documented equipment, lease terms, staff structure, and sales records
make this sellable to another operator — a straightforward, lower-capital
exit relative to a full-service restaurant.

## AI Recommendations

Prioritize AI tools for inventory demand forecasting and loyalty-program
tracking — implementation accelerators — while keeping every drink made
and every customer interaction fully staff-led.
