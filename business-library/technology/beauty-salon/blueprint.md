# Business Blueprint — Lumière Hair & Beauty Studio

_Educational business-building guidance only. This document describes how
to build and grow a small multi-chair hair and beauty salon as a
business. Each `##` heading corresponds to one of the 25 sections in
`features/blueprint`'s v2 canonical structure._

## Executive Summary

Lumière Hair & Beauty Studio is a small multi-chair hair and beauty
salon leasing a storefront, built around each stylist developing a
loyal, recurring client relationship rather than a rotating walk-in
chain experience. It's for a licensed, experienced stylist who wants to
build a small team studio, not rent a single chair solo — this is not a
solo-founder-friendly business.

## Entrepreneur Fit

Best suited to `theSpecialist` or `theOperator` archetype, with at least
three years of licensed styling experience. The strongest `creator`
entrepreneur-DNA match of any business in this library — real craft
talent matters as much as operations here.

## Business Overview

A leased storefront salon with multiple styling chairs, earning revenue
from haircutting, coloring, and styling services plus a monthly blowout
membership, growing through visual social content and client referrals.

## Market Intelligence

Demand comes from style-conscious clients who want a consistent stylist
relationship. The competitive set includes walk-in chain salons (cheaper
and more convenient, but no relationship continuity) and high-end luxury
spas (more prestige, but far more expensive).

## Customer Intelligence

Primary customer type is B2C: style-conscious professional clients aged
25-45. The core pain point is a rotating cast of unfamiliar stylists at
chain salons who don't know the client's hair history.

## Offer Architecture

The core offer is haircutting, coloring, and styling services booked
directly with a specific stylist, opening with a free consultation, plus
a monthly blowout membership for recurring maintenance visits.

## Revenue Architecture

Revenue is collected same-day at every appointment, giving fast,
predictable cash flow once the chair schedule fills — unlike the delayed
payout of a project-based professional service.

## Pricing Strategy

Per-service menu pricing (e.g. €45-65 for a cut and style, €90 for
single-process color, €140-180 for balayage), plus an optional €120/month
blowout membership covering four visits.

## Marketing System

Positioning: "your stylist, not just any stylist." Primary channel is
Instagram built on real before/after client transformations; a client
referral discount program builds secondary awareness, with local
micro-influencer partnerships as an experimental channel.

## Sales System

A free consultation at the first booking clarifies hair goals and sets
expectations before any service begins — a short, roughly 3-day
booking-to-visit cycle.

## Operations System

Core recurring processes: the first-visit consultation, chair scheduling
across stylists, retail and back-bar inventory management, and rebooking
the next appointment before the client leaves the chair.

## Technology Stack

Built on Booksy for booking and scheduling and Square for point-of-sale,
with Canva for social content design — a lighter, service-scheduling-
focused stack than the CRM-heavy stacks of B2B professional services
elsewhere in this library.

## Automation Opportunities

Online booking, appointment reminders, and rebooking prompts are
automated; AI helps plan and caption a content calendar of before/after
transformation posts.

## Team Structure

Not solo-friendly — needs at least one additional licensed stylist from
day one to support a real chair schedule; growth means recruiting
stylists who bring their own established client base.

## Financial Overview

Startup budget of roughly €10,000-€30,000 covers the storefront lease,
chairs, and initial inventory — a genuinely medium budget tier driven
mainly by the physical location; target monthly income ranges
€4,000-€12,000 with an estimated 9-month path to break-even.

## KPIs

Tracked against client rebooking rate before leaving the chair, gross
margin against product costs and commission splits, social-content-to-
booking conversion, and client churn.

## Risk Analysis

Moderate difficulty with high AI-resistance — hands-on styling work
cannot be automated. The three biggest risks are a stylist leaving and
taking their client base with them, fixed lease and staffing costs
continuing regardless of how full the schedule is, and shifting beauty
trends requiring the salon to keep technique current.

## Competitive Advantages

A consistent stylist relationship that chain salons fundamentally
cannot offer, fast same-day revenue collection compared to project-based
services elsewhere in this library, and genuinely AI-resistant,
hands-on craft work.

## Launch Strategy

Secure the storefront lease and complete buildout only once at least one
additional experienced stylist is committed, then validate pricing with
the first 15-20 appointments before the full public launch.

## 90-Day Action Plan

Weeks 1-5: secure the lease, complete buildout, and hire the first
stylist. Weeks 6-9: run a soft opening and validate pricing with the
first 15-20 appointments. Weeks 10-13: launch the Instagram content
strategy and referral program publicly.

## Scaling Strategy

Fill every chair with a stylist building a loyal recurring client base,
launch the membership program for predictable recurring revenue, then
add chairs only once existing ones are consistently full — evaluate a
second location only once the first runs profitably without daily owner
involvement.

## Exit Opportunities

A documented client base, stylist agreements, and lease terms make this
sellable to another salon operator or a stylist buyout, provided the
salon's value doesn't rest entirely on the founder's own personal chair.

## AI Recommendations

Prioritize AI tools for social-content planning and administrative
scheduling — implementation accelerators — while keeping the styling
work and client relationships fully human-led.
