# Business Blueprint — Ashgrove Software Co.

_Educational business-building guidance only. This document describes how to
build and grow a custom software development studio as a business — it is
not a substitute for a real technical due-diligence process on any specific
project. Each `##` heading corresponds to one of the 25 sections in
`features/blueprint`'s v2 canonical structure._

## Executive Summary

Ashgrove Software Co. is a custom software development studio: a small,
distributed team of engineers who build bespoke products and internal
tools for funded startups and mid-market companies on fixed-scope project
contracts, then convert delivered projects into ongoing maintenance
retainers. It's for a technically credible operator who can run delivery
discipline (sprints, demos, QA) as tightly as they can hold a client
relationship. It is not a fit for a solo developer who wants to code
full-time and avoid client management — this is a team business from the
start, not a freelance practice that happens to have a company name.

## Entrepreneur Fit

Best suited to `theBuilder` or `theOperator` archetype, with at least four
years of prior software-delivery experience. The founder doesn't need to
be the team's strongest engineer, but must understand enough of the stack
to scope a project honestly and defend an estimate to a skeptical client.

## Business Overview

A project-based custom software studio serving two buyer types: funded,
non-technical startup founders who need their first real product built,
and mid-market operations leaders who need an internal tool no off-the-shelf
SaaS product covers.

## Market Intelligence

Demand is split between founders priced out of hiring a full in-house team
before their MVP is proven, and mid-market companies whose internal-tooling
needs are too specific for horizontal SaaS. The competitive set ranges from
solo freelance developers (cheap, but capacity-constrained and risky) to
large offshore dev shops (lower per-hour rates, but weaker communication
and less delivery accountability) — a small, senior, communicative studio
sits deliberately between the two.

## Customer Intelligence

Primary customer type is B2B: funded startup founders building their first
product, and operations/product leaders at mid-market companies (50-500
employees) commissioning an internal tool. Common pain points are a
previous freelancer who disappeared mid-project, or an in-house team too
stretched to take on a new internal build.

## Offer Architecture

The core offer is a fixed-scope software build, always preceded by a paid
discovery sprint that produces the technical spec the fixed price is based
on. A post-launch maintenance retainer is offered at project completion,
not sold separately from day one.

## Revenue Architecture

Revenue is project-fee-based (milestone billing) rather than subscription
from the start, supplemented by a growing base of maintenance retainers and
occasional technical-advisory hours — a moderate-speed, lumpy ramp rather
than steady MRR.

## Pricing Strategy

Fixed-scope pricing set from a paid discovery sprint's technical spec, not
an hourly rate — this protects margin from scope creep and gives the client
budget certainty a freelancer or hourly agency can't offer.

## Marketing System

Positioning: "the engineering team funded founders and mid-market operators
hire when the project is too custom for a template and too specialized to
build in-house yet." Primary channel is referrals from investors, product
agencies, and past clients; technical content (engineering case studies) and
targeted outbound build secondary and experimental awareness.

## Sales System

A consultative, technically led sales motion: every fixed-price proposal is
preceded by a paid discovery sprint, with a roughly 45-day cycle from first
conversation to signed project.

## Operations System

Core recurring processes: a discovery-and-scoping sprint, two-week delivery
sprints with live client demos, a staging-QA pass before every release, and
a post-launch maintenance SLA.

## Technology Stack

Built on Linear for project management, GitHub for version control and
CI/CD, Figma for design handoff, and AWS for hosting — a deliberately low
level of internal-operations automation, since the product itself (software
for the client) is the automation being sold.

## Automation Opportunities

AI pair-programming tools meaningfully speed up boilerplate code, test
writing, and documentation; AI-assisted static analysis is an emerging
practice for catching bugs before human code review.

## Team Structure

Not solo-founder-friendly — this is a team business from the first client.
A senior engineer plus a project manager (often the founder, early on) is
the minimum viable delivery team.

## Financial Overview

Startup budget of roughly €15,000-€30,000 covers legal setup, tooling, and
an initial contractor retainer; target monthly income ranges
€15,000-€45,000 with an estimated 10-month path to break-even.

## KPIs

Tracked against gross margin per project, discovery-sprint-to-signed-project
close rate, project-to-retainer conversion (customer retention), and
maintenance-retainer MRR growth.

## Risk Analysis

High difficulty and moderate AI-resistance. The three biggest risks are
scope creep on fixed-price contracts, key-engineer dependency mid-project,
and AI coding assistants commoditizing simple CRUD-app work at the low end
of the market.

## Competitive Advantages

A paid discovery sprint that de-risks fixed pricing, a dedicated project
manager per client, and a two-week sprint cadence with live demos that keep
non-technical founders confident throughout the build.

## Launch Strategy

Form the company, draft reusable contract templates, then sell and deliver
a paid discovery sprint to the first real prospect before ever quoting a
fixed price — the discovery sprint IS the launch product.

## 90-Day Action Plan

Weeks 1-4: finish company formation, tooling setup, and the reusable
discovery-sprint proposal template. Weeks 5-9: sell and deliver the first
paid discovery sprint, then the first fixed-scope project's first sprint.
Weeks 10-13: complete the first project's staging-QA pass, ship it on time
and on budget, and proactively offer the maintenance retainer at handoff.

## Scaling Strategy

Scale by hiring a second senior engineer to remove key-person delivery
risk, then specialize into one or two vertical niches (logistics
back-office tools, healthtech internal systems) to command premium
fixed-scope pricing instead of competing as a generalist shop.

## Exit Opportunities

A documented client book, active maintenance-retainer contracts, and
standardized engineering playbooks are attractive to larger studios or
agencies looking to acquire a delivery team and a book of recurring
retainer revenue.

## AI Recommendations

Prioritize AI tools for accelerating implementation (pair-programming,
test generation, code review) rather than for technical scoping or client
communication — the discovery-sprint judgment call and the client
relationship stay human-led even as boilerplate coding automates.
